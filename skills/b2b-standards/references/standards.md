# The Six B2B Standards (v1.0)

The minimum standards for building a fast-growing sales-led B2B startup. They apply if you are selling enterprise or SMB, AI or SaaS. They apply before you have hired in sales and after. They even apply before you have a product.

## Why these exist

There are two kinds of early-stage B2B startup:

1. The promising startup that can become an outlier. They have a chance to hit hypergrowth and do not want to miss it, but are not sure they are doing everything they need to do to nail it.
2. The startup that does not realize it is dying. It looks like it is on the path to hypergrowth. It very much is not.

Very few founders can tell which one they are. Real revenue and Fortune 500 logos do not tell you. A founder can have both and still discover, in a single week, that his pipeline is mostly fake and his company is going to die.

The fix is a standard for what good looks like, defined so you cannot weasel out of it, so you cannot convince yourself you are doing OK when you are not. That is what this file is.

Startups, by design, want to grow fast. Not growing fast means something is wrong. If you are not hitting these minimum standards, something is wrong. Hitting these minimum standards is the ONLY priority in the early stage.

## How to read the standards

- **You must hit all six.** Hitting five means you have not met the standard. No partial credit.
- **This is the floor, not the ceiling.** This is the 1.0 standard that earns you the right to attack 2.0 and 3.0, where hypergrowth is found. Clearing it is the starting point, not the finish line.
- **The numbers vary by initial contract size.** Three bands, used throughout:
  - **Big contracts:** $100k+
  - **Moderate contracts:** $25k to $99k
  - **Small contracts:** sub-$25k
- Consider it tough love. The alternative is realizing six months too late that you could have course-corrected.

## The six standards

### 1. Repeatable Case Study

A startup is a case study factory: it produces near-clones of a single case study. The repeatable case study is the foundation for go-to-market, product, and scale.

You must have a SINGLE repeatable case study. Specifically: one situation where it would be weird if the person in the case study did anything OTHER than buy your product right now.

- If they could DIY it with Claude Code, it would be weird if they bought from you.
- If they could delay buying, it would be weird if they bought from you.
- If they could do nothing, it would be weird if they bought from you.

Write it in the PULL framework (see `pull-framework.md`): their Project, why it is Unavoidable right now, the List of options they have tried and each one's Limitations, and the smallest version of your supply that unblocks them. Then ask: would it be weird if this person did not buy from us right now?

**Bar:** one repeatable case study that passes the weird-not-to-buy test. Binary. You have it or you do not.

### 2. Pipeline

First meetings per week with new potential customers. Most startups are bottlenecked on pipeline, on meeting enough new customers. Few treat it as a bottleneck.

**Bar (per person selling, by contract size):**

- Big ($100k+): **3 first meetings per week**
- Moderate ($25k to $99k): **10 first meetings per week**
- Small (sub-$25k): **15 first meetings per week**

(For calibration: before Tabs even had a product, each cofounder was scheduling 3 to 4 meetings per day.)

### 3. Pull

When customers have PULL, they try to buy. "Pulling" means "trying to buy." The potential customer initiates a trying-to-buy-shaped action:

- Selling to a startup or small business: they try to buy on the call. They ask for the Stripe link and schedule an onboarding call.
- Selling into a bigger company: they almost certainly will not buy right away, but they do something close to it. "OK, I need this, and here is what needs to happen so I can bring this into my organization." Then they walk you through who needs to approve and they initiate the deal's next steps.

The full definition of a pull action (and what does not count, like seller-prompted next steps) is in `pull-framework.md`. Grade individual calls with `call-review.md`.

**Bar:** **80% of first meetings have pull.** Meeting with people who do not have pull is a bug in your GTM and a waste of your time. Once you have a repeatable case study, your first sales call is about disqualifying people who do not fit it. Or rather, having them convince you they fit it.

### 4. Close Rate

Of your sales meetings where there is pull, the share that become signed customers.

**Bar:** **50% of pull meetings close.** (At some point you will optimize pricing and might accept a lower close rate for higher added revenue. Think of that like dedicated calf training when you are just trying to squat 315. Not yet.)

### 5. Cycle Time

Time from a **meeting with pull** to a signed contract.

**Bar (by contract size):**

- Big ($100k+): **90 days**
- Moderate ($25k to $99k): **30 days**
- Small (sub-$25k): **14 days**

There could be caveats here for pilots, trials, and land-and-expand motions. There are not.

### 6. Success Rate

When a customer buys from you, they are guaranteed to churn, until X happens, in which case it would be weird if they churned.

X is your leading indicator of retention. Most startups do not know what X is. Worse, many think X is "usage" or "ROI" or "satisfaction/NPS," when it is almost never those things. Far too many 12-month contracts blindside the founder in month 11.

**Bar:** **95% of new customers hit X within 1 month of purchase.** If you cannot name your X, you cannot hit this standard, and the unnamed X is usually itself the problem.

## How to attack the standards

Review your performance against all six in a weekly ritual where you stare reality in the face and ask: what do I need to change next week? That ritual is `weekly-review.md`.

Do not expect the standards to be easy, or hitting them to be fast. There are cheat codes (finding intense pull, or someone on your cap table who solves pipeline with a ton of intros), but even with a cheat code, hitting the standard is brutally difficult, takes all your time and energy, and a ton of iteration. You will take steps forward and steps backward. Such is the game.
